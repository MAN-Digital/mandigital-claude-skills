# Writing for a Non-Native Speaker to Read Aloud

The presenter is not a native English speaker. Every sentence must be easy to say out loud
on the first take — that's a stricter bar than "reads fine on the page."

## The core rule

One idea per sentence. Target 8–15 words. Hard cap 20. If a sentence needs "and," "which,"
or "because" **plus** a dash, split it into two or three sentences instead.

## Before / after

These are real over-complex sentences a first-draft attempt at this exact task produced,
rewritten to the spoken standard:

❌ Too complex (28 words, three embedded clauses):
"If any one of those four is a mess — no clean discount property, deals with inconsistent
amounts, no defined quote stage — the workflow you build on top of it will be fragile."

✅ Rewritten:
"If any of these four things is messy, the workflow breaks. A messy discount property.
Inconsistent deal amounts. No clear quote stage. Fix the data first."

❌ Too complex (34 words, buried conditional plus an aside):
"That's a meaningful detail if you've ever dealt with an enterprise buyer's IT team blocking
access to a vendor tool, or a stakeholder who just never gets around to creating an account
for something they'll only use twice."

✅ Rewritten:
"This matters for one reason. Enterprise IT teams often block vendor logins. Buyers skip
creating an account they'll only use twice. A shared link removes both problems."

## No rhetorical or audience-directed questions

The blocklist bans "language that implies questions." Never ask the viewer anything, even
rhetorically.

❌ "Sound familiar?"
❌ "Pretty slick, right?"
✅ State the fact and move on: "That's not a sales problem. That's a missing approval
step."

## No hedging, no conditionals

Zero: might, could, may, perhaps, seems to, tends to, "imagine if," "what if." State the
claim directly. If something is genuinely subject to change (a HubSpot UI label, for
example), say so as a fact — "HubSpot renames this occasionally, check your portal" — not
as a hedge.

## No passive voice

"The workflow is triggered by..." → "The workflow triggers when..."

## Watch triplet overuse

"X, Y, and Z" lists are a known AI-writing tic, and they're also harder to deliver smoothly
out loud than they look on paper. Use at most one triplet per script section; vary with
single points, pairs, or short numbered lists instead.

## Sounds, not just spelling

Read the line aloud before finalizing. Avoid stacking similar consonant sounds across word
boundaries ("six sales stages," "strict script structure") and long strings of unstressed
syllables. If it trips when you say it, rewrite it.

## Numbers

Write numbers the way they're spoken: "twenty percent," not "20%." Exception: on-screen
text callouts can use digits and symbols, since those are read, not spoken.
