# Hook Frameworks - Complete Guide

## The 3 Core Hook Frameworks

These frameworks are your foundation for creating scroll-stopping LinkedIn hooks. Master them.

---

## 1. TAS Framework

**Thesis → Antithesis → Synthesis**

### Structure

- **Thesis:** Present an opinion or bold claim
- **Antithesis:** Recognize a barrier or counterargument
- **Synthesis:** Share a way through

### When to Use

- Contrarian takes
- Thought leadership content
- Challenging conventional wisdom
- Industry commentary
- Reframing common beliefs

### Why It Works

TAS is uniquely effective at presenting an opinionated or bold claim (thesis) and a counter-argument or reaction (antithesis) that drives home a better way of thinking or doing things (synthesis).

### TAS Patterns

**Pattern 1: Opinion + Bad Take Pattern**

```
Thesis: [Opinion] + stance on [bad take]

Antithesis: [Barrier standing in the way]

Synthesis: I've spent [social proof + timeframe] to achieve [outcome]. The playbook:
```

**Real Example:**
```
Content marketing is broken.

Most teams chase virality over value. They optimize for clicks, not conversions.

But here's the problem: viral content attracts the wrong audience.

After building content systems for 50+ B2B companies, I've learned: targeted content that converts 10 people beats viral content that converts none.

The playbook:
```

**Pattern 2: Common Practice is Wrong Pattern**

```
Thesis: [Common practice] is [bold contrarian opinion]

Antithesis: I've seen [common practice] in [format]. [Why it fails]

Synthesis: Do [this better idea] instead
```

**Real Example:**
```
Most cold outreach is spam disguised as personalization.

I've seen it everywhere: "Hey [First Name], I noticed [Generic Thing About Company]..."

The problem? Everyone uses the same playbook. Your prospects see through it instantly.

Do this instead: Research one specific problem they actually have. Reference it with evidence. Offer a solution.

Here's the framework:
```

**Pattern 3: Industry Standard Challenge Pattern**

```
Thesis: [Standard approach] doesn't work anymore

Antithesis: The market shifted but most people haven't

Synthesis: Here's what works now:
```

**Real Example:**
```
Traditional sales demos don't close deals anymore.

The market shifted. Buyers now do 83% of research before talking to sales. They don't want a demo. They want proof you understand their problem.

Here's what works now:
```

```

**Template 2:**
```

```

**Template 3:**
```

## 2. PAS Framework

**Problem → Agitate → Solution**

### Structure

- **Problem:** Identify the challenge upfront
- **Agitate:** Poke the pain to increase relatability
- **Solution:** Present your solution

### When to Use

- Pain-focused content
- Solution selling
- Addressing specific struggles
- Product/service positioning
- Empathy-driven posts

### Why It Works

This framework builds emotional resonance by spiking readers' feelings. When you agitate a problem, you make it impossible to ignore. Ideally, readers feel compelled to continue reading because they're eager to solve the problem you've brought to light.

### PAS Patterns

**Pattern 1: Everyone Struggles Pattern**

```
Problem: Everybody [pain point]

Agitate: I used to [pain point], too. [Example]

Solution: Here are X mistakes I made (and how I'd fix them today):
```

**Real Example:**
```
Everybody struggles with LinkedIn consistency.

I used to post once, get crickets, then disappear for three months. Rinse and repeat. The guilt built up every time I logged in.

Here are 5 mistakes I made (and how I'd fix them today):
```

**Pattern 2: It Sucks When Pattern**

```
Problem: It sucks when [problem causes issues]

Agitate: It's even worse when [problem] could have been solved by [small change]

Solution: [This solution] can fix [problem]
```

**Real Example:**
```
It sucks when you lose a deal to a competitor.

It's even worse when you find out they won because of a feature you already have. You just didn't communicate it clearly.

Here's how to fix your positioning:
```

**Pattern 3: Do You Pattern**

```
Problem: Do you [situation that causes pain]?

Agitate: You're [details on how you're exacerbating pain]

Solution: Here's how to [overcome pain and change for the better]
```

**Real Example:**
```
Do you find yourself rewriting the same cold emails over and over?

You're wasting 10+ hours weekly on repetitive tasks while your response rates stay flat.

Here's how to build an email system that scales:
```

**Pattern 4: Opening Line + Spiky Take Pattern**

```
Problem: [Opening line to grab attention]. [Spiky take that stops you]

Agitate: [Do the opposite of what you're doing]

Solution: Here's how to [change your mindset and actions] to [get what you want]
```

**Real Example:**
```
Want to grow on LinkedIn? Stop posting every day.

Consistency without strategy is just noise. You're training the algorithm that your content doesn't matter.

Here's how to post less but grow faster:
```

### PAS Hook Templates

**Template 1:**
```
[Pain point statement]

Most people try [common solution]. It doesn't work because [reason].

Here's what does work:
```

**Template 2:**
```
The biggest mistake [audience] make with [topic]:

They [wrong action] before they [right action].

Here's the correct sequence:
```

**Template 3:**
```
If you're [struggling with X], you're not alone.

[Agitate with specific consequences and scale]

The fix is simpler than you think:
```

## 3. SCQA Framework

**Situation → Complication → Question → Answer**

### Structure

- **Situation:** Set the stage (touch on current state)
- **Complication:** The challenge this presents
- **Question:** The bridge between problem and solution
- **Answer:** The resolution (dangle the solution)

### When to Use

- Educational content
- Industry insights
- Complex topics that need context
- Framework/methodology posts
- Data-driven content

### Why It Works

The goal is to pique the reader's interest so that when they reach the answer, they're eager to read more to learn if your solution will remedy the problem or teach them something new.

### SCQA Patterns

**Pattern 1: Opinionated State of Play Pattern**

```
Situation: [Opinionated state of play]. [Data]

Complication: [Poke at pain]

Question: What to do?

Answer: [Opinionated solution]. Here's how:
```

**Real Example:**
```
Two million articles are published daily on the web.

Almost none use storytelling properly. They confuse anecdotes with story structure. The result? Forgettable content.

What to do?

Learn the 5 elements of story that make content memorable:
```

**Pattern 2: Fear Overcoming Pattern**

```
Situation: Top reason people don't [execute a thing]

Complication: [Emphasize fear]

Question/Statement: You can overcome [fear] with [alternate option]

Answer: [Alternate solution]:
```

**Real Example:**
```
Top reason people don't create content: "I don't know what to say"

The fear of sounding stupid keeps brilliant people silent. Meanwhile, average advice gets millions of views.

You can overcome imposter syndrome with a simple framework:

The 3x3 Content Matrix:
```

**Pattern 3: Convincing Others Pattern**

```
Situation: Convincing [audience segment] to invest in [thing] is hard

Complication: [Audience segment] wants to see [results]

Question/Statement: Feeling frustrated?

Answer: Here's how to make a case:
```

**Real Example:**
```

They want immediate ROI. Content takes 6-12 months to compound. The mismatch kills budgets.

Feeling frustrated?

Here's how to make a case using their language:
```

**Pattern 4: Two Opposites Pattern**

```
Situation: The [difference between two opposites]: [Answer]

Complication: [Getting this wrong can ruin the thing]

Question/Statement: N/A (not always necessary)

Answer: X frameworks to [get it right]
```

**Real Example:**
```

Copywriting sells. Content educates. Mixing them up confuses your audience and tanks conversions.

```

## Advanced Hook Techniques

### Technique 1: The Data Hook

Start with a surprising statistic:

```
[Shocking percentage] of [audience] [surprising behavior]

This number should [emotion] every [target persona].

Here's what it means:
```

**Example:**
```
89% of B2B buyers research vendors online before making contact.

This number should change how you think about content marketing.

Here's what it means:
```

### Technique 2: The Transformation Hook

Lead with dramatic change:

```
From [starting point] to [end point] in [timeframe].

Here's exactly what changed:
```

**Example:**
```
From 50 to 5,000 email subscribers in 6 months.

Here's exactly what changed:
```

### Technique 3: The Pattern Hook

Share observed trends:

```
I've noticed something interesting across [number]+ [work/analysis]:

The ones that [succeed] have [common trait].

Here's the pattern:
```

**Example:**
```
I've noticed something across 200+ HubSpot audits:

The companies with the best attribution have the simplest tracking setups.

Here's the pattern:
```

### Technique 4: The Conversation Hook

Use dialogue to engage:

```
This [timeframe] conversation changed how I think about [topic] forever.

[Person]: "[Challenge/objection]"

Me: "You're right. Here's why:"
```

**Example:**
```
This 30-second conversation changed my pricing forever.

Client: "Your proposal is 3x more expensive than your competitor."

Me: "You're right. Here's why:"
```

## Hook Quality Checklist

Rate every hook you create on these 8 criteria:

### 1. Specific (not generic)
- [ ] Uses actual numbers, not "many" or "lots"
- [ ] Names specific problems, not vague challenges
- [ ] References concrete outcomes, not generic benefits

❌ Generic: "I've worked with many companies"
✅ Specific: "I've audited 200+ HubSpot implementations"

### 2. Contrarian or Unexpected
- [ ] Challenges conventional wisdom
- [ ] Presents surprising angle
- [ ] Goes against common practice

❌ Expected: "Content marketing is important"
✅ Contrarian: "Most content marketing is wasted effort"

### 3. Emotional (creates feeling)
- [ ] Pokes at pain
- [ ] Inspires or motivates
- [ ] Creates curiosity or urgency

❌ Flat: "Attribution is useful"
✅ Emotional: "Flying blind on revenue is terrifying"

### 4. Relatable (audience sees themselves)
- [ ] Addresses their specific situation
- [ ] Uses their language
- [ ] Reflects their struggles

❌ Distant: "Companies struggle with systems"
✅ Relatable: "You're spending 20 hours weekly on manual reporting"

### 5. Data-driven (when relevant)
- [ ] Includes credibility markers
- [ ] References specific experience
- [ ] Backs claims with evidence

❌ Vague: "I have experience with this"
✅ Credible: "After 200+ audits, here's what I've learned"

### 6. Cliffhanger (opens a loop)
- [ ] Doesn't give everything away
- [ ] Creates desire to read more
- [ ] Promises valuable information

❌ Complete: "Here's how I did it: [full explanation]"
✅ Cliffhanger: "Here's what changed everything:"

### 7. Mobile-friendly (scans well)
- [ ] Short sentences
- [ ] Clear formatting
- [ ] Easy to read while scrolling

❌ Dense: Long run-on sentence that goes on and on
✅ Scannable: Short. Punchy. Clear.

### 8. Authentic (sounds like you)
- [ ] Not templated or robotic
- [ ] Reflects your voice
- [ ] Feels natural

❌ Robotic: "Leverage synergistic methodologies"
✅ Authentic: "Stop overcomplicating it"

## Hook Testing Process

**Step 1: Create Multiple Versions**
- Minimum 4-5 hooks per nugget
- Use all 3 frameworks (TAS, PAS, SCQA)
- Try different angles

**Step 2: Evaluate Each Hook**
- Apply the 8-point quality checklist
- Score each hook 1-10 on "stop-scroll" power
- Be honest—would YOU stop scrolling?

**Step 3: Select Best 2**
- Choose hooks that score highest
- Ensure they're different enough (not redundant)
- Verify they support different post angles

**Step 4: Refine Selected Hooks**
- Polish wording
- Test mobile preview
- Read aloud for flow
- Ensure authenticity

## Common Hook Mistakes to Avoid

### Mistake 1: Starting with a Question
```
❌ "Do you struggle with LinkedIn?"
✅ "73% of B2B professionals waste 10+ hours weekly on LinkedIn content that gets ignored"
```

Questions in hooks often fall flat. Lead with statements.

### Mistake 2: Being Too Clever/Cryptic
```
❌ "The secret they don't want you to know..."
✅ "After 200+ audits, here's the #1 HubSpot mistake"
```

Clarity beats cleverness. Every time.

### Mistake 3: No Credibility Marker
```
❌ "Here's how to improve attribution"
✅ "After tracking $100M in revenue, here's what actually drives attribution accuracy"
```

Give readers a reason to trust you.

### Mistake 4: Vague Outcomes
```
❌ "Get better results with HubSpot"
✅ "Cut 15 hours of manual reporting weekly with this HubSpot setup"
```

Specific outcomes make hooks compelling.

### Mistake 5: Clickbait Without Substance
```
❌ "You won't believe what happened next..."
✅ "This 30-second conversation changed my pricing strategy forever"
```

Intrigue is good. Deception is bad.

### Mistake 6: Too Long
```
❌ [8-sentence hook explaining everything]
✅ [2-4 sentence hook that opens a loop]
```

Hooks should tease, not tell everything.

### Mistake 7: Generic and Boring
```
❌ "Social media is important for B2B"
✅ "Most B2B companies waste $50K+ yearly on social media that generates zero pipeline"
```

Specific and bold beats generic and safe.

## Hook Writing Exercises

### Exercise 1: Framework Rotation

Take one nugget. Write it using:
- TAS Framework (2 versions)
- PAS Framework (1 version)
- SCQA Framework (1 version)

Compare the 4 hooks. Which is strongest?

### Exercise 2: Specificity Challenge

Take a generic hook and make it specific:

```
Generic: "Content marketing can help your business"
↓
Specific: "Content marketing drove 73% of our $5M pipeline last year"
↓
More Specific: "One blog post generated $500K in pipeline. Here's the framework we used:"
```

### Exercise 3: Contrarian Reframe

Take a common belief. Challenge it:

```
Common: "Post consistently to grow on LinkedIn"
↓
Contrarian: "Posting every day without strategy is training the algorithm to ignore you"
```

### Exercise 4: Mobile Preview

Write a hook. View it on mobile. Ask:
- Does it scan quickly?
- Are sentences too long?
- Does it stop your scroll?

Rewrite until it passes mobile test.

## Framework Selection Guide

**When to use TAS:**
- You have a contrarian opinion
- You're challenging industry norms
- You want to establish thought leadership
- Your content is opinion-driven

**When to use PAS:**
- You're addressing a specific pain
- You have a solution to share
- Your audience is problem-aware
- You want to build empathy first

**When to use SCQA:**
- You're teaching something
- The topic needs context
- You're sharing a methodology
- Your audience needs education

**When to use Hybrid:**
- You're comfortable with frameworks
- You want maximum creativity
- The topic doesn't fit neatly
- You have strong voice/style
