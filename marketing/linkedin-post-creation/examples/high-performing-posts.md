# High-Performing LinkedIn Posts - Analysis & Examples

## Purpose

This document analyzes real high-performing B2B LinkedIn posts to understand what works and why.

## Example 1: Erica Schneider - Contrarian Take

**Hook:**
```
Every other post I read: "I quit my full-time job X months ago and now I'm blissfully blissful cuz freedom. Oh, and I'm already making $100K."

Sheesh. These artificial piles of poop posts are fully unrealistic and also very boring.
```

**Why It Works:**

1. **Specific** - Uses fake quote with specific quantifier ($100K)
2. **Contrarian** - Loudly dissents common format and topic
3. **Emotional** - Gets you nodding along, thinking "right?!"
4. **Relatable** - If you feel the same way, you're keen to engage

**Framework Used:** TAS (Thesis: These posts are artificial, Antithesis: Everyone posts them, Synthesis: Here's the truth)

**Engagement Drivers:**
- Spiky point of view
- Calls out behavior directly
- Creates "finally someone said it!" reaction
- Polarizing (intentionally)

**Key Lesson:** Don't be afraid to call out what annoys you (and your audience). Strong opinions spark strong engagement.

## Example 2: Matt Swain - Hook System

**Hook:**
```
I've reviewed 3500+ posts that generated $50M+ in revenue.

Here's the hook framework I give marketing teams:

1. Lead with transformation + numbers
   "From 100 to 10,000 customers in 12 months"

2. Show, don't tell your expertise
   "After scaling 50+ SaaS companies from $1M to $10M ARR..."
```

**Why It Works:**

1. **Credibility** - "3500+ posts" and "$50M" establish authority
2. **Specific outcome** - Framework for creating hooks
3. **Immediate value** - Starts teaching right away
4. **Scannable** - Numbered format easy to digest

**Framework Used:** Hook Framework System (meta—teaching hooks with a hook!)

**Engagement Drivers:**
- High credibility markers
- Actionable immediately
- Easy to save and refer back
- Educational value

**Key Lesson:** Lead with impressive numbers that establish credibility, then deliver on the promise immediately.

## Example 3: Katelyn Bourgoin - Data + Story

**Hook:**
```
Katelyn is in the business of knowing why customers buy. So when she says she's spent 1500+ hours learning about it, you believe her.

She doesn't simply explain concepts; she shows you good vs. bad examples of every one. And cleverly leaves a takeaway or lesson at the end of each, too.
```

**Why It Works:**

1. **Credibility** - 1500+ hours is specific and impressive
2. **Format innovation** - Good vs. bad examples
3. **Thoughtful structure** - Takeaway at end of each section
4. **Skimmable** - Can read headers and takeaways only

**Framework Used:** Educational listicle with comparison format

**Engagement Drivers:**
- Examples make it concrete
- Comparisons help understanding
- Skimmers still get value
- Actionable takeaways

**Key Lesson:** Show, don't tell. Good vs. bad examples are more powerful than abstract advice.

## Example 4: Chima Mmeje - Story Format

**Hook:**
```
One year ago...

Behind the scenes: What it's really like building a content agency

What you see: Polished case studies and happy clients

What you don't see: The 3am calls with stressed founders
```

**Why It Works:**

1. **Timeframe** - "One year ago" makes it easy to picture
2. **Contrast** - "What you see" vs "What you don't see"
3. **Honest** - Shares the messy reality
4. **Relatable** - Everyone knows polished ≠ reality

**Framework Used:** Story with behind-the-scenes reveal

**Engagement Drivers:**
- Authenticity breeds connection
- Contrast creates interest
- Vulnerability is magnetic
- Relatable struggle

**Key Lesson:** Share the messy reality behind the polished exterior. Vulnerability beats perfection.

## Example 6: Before & After Hook Comparison

**❌ Bad Hook (April 2022):**
```
Thread

Storytelling is becoming a buzzword.

You jet storytelling in. JET storytelling out.

We need to bring value back into focus.

How does it work? How is it different from anecdotes? How should businesses use it?
```

**Problems:**
- "Thread" label (outdated trend)
- Confusing first sentence (assumes context)
- "JET storytelling" makes no sense
- No clear value proposition
- Questions without answers
- Terrible hook—doesn't stop scroll

**✅ Good Hook (June 2022):**
```
Two million articles are published every day on the web.

Almost none use story properly.

Storytelling: 5 steps to be more persuasive, interesting, and memorable.
```

**Improvements:**
- Removed "Thread" label
- Specific data point (2 million)
- Clear problem (almost none use story properly)
- Specific outcome (persuasive, interesting, memorable)
- No assumptions—stands alone
- Immediate value promise

**Key Lesson:** Huge improvement in just 2 months. Your early posts will be bad. Keep improving.

## Example 7: Wes Kao - Problem Agitation

**Hook:**
```
Want to grow on LinkedIn? Stop posting every day.

Consistency without strategy is just noise. You're training the algorithm that your content doesn't matter.

Here's how to post less but grow faster:
```

**Why It Works:**

1. **Counterintuitive** - Goes against "post daily" advice
2. **Problem framing** - "Consistency without strategy is noise"
3. **Clear villain** - The algorithm
4. **Promise** - Post less, grow faster

**Framework Used:** PAS (Problem: posting daily doesn't work, Agitate: you're training algorithm wrong, Solution: here's better approach)

**Engagement Drivers:**
- Contrarian advice
- Relieves guilt (don't have to post daily)
- Clear benefit
- Curious about solution

**Key Lesson:** Challenge conventional wisdom when you have evidence it's wrong.

## What High-Performing Posts Have in Common

### 1. Strong Hooks

Every high-performing post has a hook that:
- Stops the scroll immediately
- Makes a promise (explicit or implicit)
- Contains credibility or specificity
- Creates curiosity or emotion

### 2. Mobile-Friendly Formatting

- One sentence per line
- Spaces between sentences
- Short sentences (mostly 10-15 words)
- Scannable structure

### 3. Clear Value Proposition

Within first 2-3 sentences, reader knows:
- What this is about
- Why they should care
- What they'll get

### 4. Authentic Voice

- Sounds like a human wrote it
- Not templated or robotic
- Personality comes through
- Opinions expressed clearly

### 5. Specific, Not Generic

- Uses real numbers
- Names real companies/people
- Describes specific situations
- Avoids vague language

### 6. Strong POV

- Has an opinion
- Willing to disagree
- Challenges something
- Not vanilla or safe

### 7. Engagement Element

- Asks a question
- Invites comments
- Creates discussion
- Makes reader want to respond

## What Doesn't Work

### 1. Generic Inspirational Posts

```
❌ "Believe in yourself and you can achieve anything!"
```

Why it fails:
- No specificity
- No credibility
- No actionable insight
- Could apply to anyone

### 2. Pure Self-Promotion

```
❌ "Check out our new feature! It's amazing! Link in comments!"
```

Why it fails:
- No value for reader
- No context or story
- Feels salesy
- No reason to care

### 3. Asking for Engagement Directly

```
❌ "Double tap if you agree! Comment 'yes' below!"
```

Why it fails:
- LinkedIn penalizes engagement bait
- Feels manipulative
- No substance
- Damages credibility

### 4. Wall of Text

```
❌ [Long paragraph without breaks, no formatting, no structure, just words words words...]
```

Why it fails:
- Not mobile-friendly
- Hard to scan
- Overwhelming
- Most people skip

### 5. No Hook

```
❌ "Today I want to talk about attribution. It's important. Here are some thoughts..."
```

Why it fails:
- Doesn't stop scroll
- No reason to keep reading
- Boring opening
- No promise of value

## Engagement Metrics That Matter

**Remember the hierarchy:**

1. **Comments** (Most valuable)
   - Indicates real engagement
   - Drives algorithm visibility
   - Builds relationships

2. **Shares/Reposts** (High value)
   - Expands reach exponentially
   - Shows content resonated
   - Builds authority

3. **Profile Views** from target audience
   - Indicates curiosity about you
   - Potential for connections
   - Quality over quantity matters

4. **Connection Requests** from ICP
   - Direct business potential
   - Audience building
   - Network effects

5. **DMs** (High intent)
   - Strongest signal of interest
   - Business opportunities
   - Relationship building

6. **Likes** (Least important)
   - Vanity metric
   - Low effort engagement
   - Don't optimize for these

**100 engaged followers > 10,000 passive followers**

## Lessons from Failed Posts

### Failed Post 1: Too Clever

**What was tried:**
```
"The secret sauce isn't sauce at all. It's vinegar. 🤔"

[Abstract metaphor about business...]
```

**Why it failed:**
- Too cryptic
- Metaphor didn't land
- No clear value
- Tried too hard to be clever

**Lesson:** Clarity beats cleverness. Every time.

### Failed Post 2: No POV

**What was tried:**
```
"Attribution is important.

There are many models.

Choose what works for you."
```

**Why it failed:**
- Vanilla content
- No opinion
- States obvious things
- No value added

**Lesson:** Strong POV required. Vanilla content gets ignored.

### Failed Post 3: Wrong Audience

**What was tried:**
```
[Highly technical deep-dive into attribution mathematics]
```

**Why it failed:**
- Too technical for LinkedIn audience
- No practical application
- Intimidating, not helpful
- Wrong platform

**Lesson:** Know your audience. LinkedIn wants practical, not academic.
